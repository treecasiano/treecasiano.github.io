Learn All the Node!!!!!! is the battle cry for 2016. I ended 2015 by attending the Node Interactive Conference in Portland after scoring a free ticket from Women Who Code (mille grazie). The conference inspired me to level up my Node skills with an increased ferocity. I was delighted to see so many applications of Node, so many companies enthusiastically incorporating Node into their tech stacks, particularly in the enterprise environment. [Atwood's law](http://blog.codinghorror.com/the-principle-of-least-power/)really is true.

## Node Interactive
I attended most of the two-day conference, my participation being slightly impeded by the flooding engendered by a ridiculously wet early December. My favorite presentations were 1) Philip James' [Building Twitter Bots in Node](https://youtu.be/xkNOKSNSoVI?list=PLfMzBWSH11xYjL8oFumSfzOf6-kr8_t-o)talk in which he referred to Twitter bots as a form of art and 2) the presentation by Mariko Kosaka [Node.js While Crafting](https://youtu.be/lLvRmjVVkTY?list=PLfMzBWSH11xYjL8oFumSfzOf6-kr8_t-o) where she discusses how she used Node and an old knitting machine to convert graphics into knitted fabric.

The presentations can all be found on[YouTube](https://youtu.be/O28Knn00qYo?list=PLfMzBWSH11xYjL8oFumSfzOf6-kr8_t-o)and the[slides are also available online](http://events.linuxfoundation.org/events/node-interactive/program/slides). I highly recommend the entire collection of event videos to anyone interested in the state of Node development. I learned that the Node ecosystem is much bigger and more interesting than I realized and it's growing at a seemingly exponential rate.

 As a side note, I was one of only a handful of women at this massive event. This fact didn't bother me at all. In fact, I found it pretty great because the women's bathrooms were pristine and usually empty.

## DIY Node-based Code School

Following the conference, I joined forces with a friend to create our own Node-focused bootcamp. We are meeting several times a week, studying and coding in the comfort of my living room, seated on my comfortable purple couches. We are also working independently on tasks in between our meetings, using Trello to keep us organized and accountable. The key things we are focusing on this month include MongoDB, general JS algorithm practice, Bootstrap, and job hunting. We're using a combination of Treehouse, Free Code Camp, NodeSchool, some books from Packt Publishing, a MongoDb course for Node developers from Mongo University, a [GitHub repo full of Front End Dev questions](https://github.com/h5bp/Front-end-Developer-Interview-Questions), and YouTube tutorials.

We're also creating a MEAN Stack app together, using the project to guide much of our
learning. We know we want to build the API first, so we are putting extra emphasis on
MongoDB training and best practices for designing an app. Coding together has
accelerated our progress considerably. It seems like whenever I am stuck on something, she is able to help me through it, and vice versa. We are fueling ourselves with quesadillas and caffeine.

Never code hungry or alone!
